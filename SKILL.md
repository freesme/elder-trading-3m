## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

**3M: Mind, Method, Money**  
Use 3M as the minimum viability test for any trading approach. `Mind` is emotional control and discipline; `Method` is a tested decision process for selecting, entering, and exiting trades; `Money` is risk management. A missing leg invalidates the whole setup: strong analysis does not compensate for poor sizing, and perfect sizing does not rescue a negative-expectancy method.

**Trade Only for the Right Reason**  
Use this as a self-check before every order: the only valid reason to trade is to make money under a tested plan. Trading for stimulation, escape, revenge, social proof, or certainty-seeking is gambling behavior. The cure is written rules, smaller size, and records that expose the motive.

**Investment, Trading, Gambling Separation**  
Classify the act before entering. Investing relies on long-term value and patience; trading exploits price swings with a plan; gambling seeks excitement or payoff without a durable edge. Never enter as a trade and then convert a loss into an "investment" because the stop was hit.

**Crowd Psychology Edge**  
Elder treats price as the crowd's momentary consensus of value. Technical analysis is useful because charts and indicators reveal changes in crowd emotion. The edge comes from staying rational when the crowd is excited, frightened, or bored.

**Triple Screen Trading System**  
Use multiple timeframes to separate strategy from tactics. First screen: choose the strategic direction on the longer timeframe with trend-following tools. Second screen: find tactical pullbacks or counter-moves on the intermediate timeframe with oscillators. Third screen: use a precise entry technique and predefined stop. Do not let a short-term signal override the long-term screen.

**Five-Bullet Technical Toolkit**  
Use a compact indicator set rather than stacking tools. Elder emphasizes moving averages, price channels, MACD histogram, Force Index, and a fifth chosen oscillator such as Elder-ray or stochastic. Combine trend-following indicators with oscillators because each fails in different market states.

**Divergence as Energy Loss**  
Use divergence to detect when a move is losing force. A new price high with a weaker indicator high warns that bulls are weakening; a new price low with a shallower indicator low warns that bears are weakening. Divergence is strongest when aligned with the higher-timeframe setup and risk rules.

**2% Rule: Single-Trade Survival**  
Never risk more than 2% of trading capital on one trade. Calculate risk from entry to stop, including commissions and slippage, then size downward. If the stop required by the chart makes the position too large for 2%, skip the trade.

**6% Rule: Monthly Circuit Breaker**  
Stop opening new risk when realized losses plus open trade risk reach 6% of the previous month-end equity. Close risk if the account breaches the monthly loss limit. This rule prevents a sequence of ordinary losses from becoming career damage.

**Business Risk vs. Loss**  
Treat small predefined losses as business expenses. A true loss is any breach of the risk budget or plan. The goal is not to avoid losing trades; the goal is to prevent losses large enough to damage survival, discipline, or decision quality.

**SafeZone Stops**  
Use SafeZone to place stops outside normal market noise. Estimate the market's recent adverse excursions, multiply by a chosen factor, and trail the stop so it follows the trend without sitting at obvious support or resistance where weak hands cluster.

**Records Are the Discipline Engine**  
Maintain four records: trade spreadsheet, equity curve, trade diary, and next-day action plan. Records are not accounting chores; they create feedback. If records are poor, the trader is probably avoiding the evidence needed to improve.

**Equity Curve as Self-Diagnosis**  
Use the equity curve as the objective measure of synchronization with the market. A smooth rising curve supports normal size; a jagged or declining curve means reduce size, review records, and stop blaming market noise.

**ABC Time Management**  
Classify watched markets weekly. `A` means tradeable tomorrow, `B` means possible this week, `C` means not actionable. Spend attention on A names, monitor B names, ignore C names until conditions change.

**Plan Before the Open**  
Make trading decisions after the market closes, while emotions are lower. Write the weekly picture, daily picture, entry, stop, target, and order instructions. During the session, execute the plan instead of improvising.
