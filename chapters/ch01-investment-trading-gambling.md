# Chapter 1: 投资，交易，还是赌博

## Core Idea

Before any market action, classify what game you are playing. Elder's first filter is behavioral: an investor, a trader, and a gambler may use the same instrument, but they differ in edge, time horizon, discipline, and exit logic.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Three Roads: investment, trading, gambling**
  - When to use: before entering any position or reviewing a losing one.
  - How: define the holding period, source of edge, exit rule, and risk limit. If these are absent or emotional, treat the action as gambling.
- **Momentum vs. countertrend trading**
  - When to use: selecting a setup type.
  - How: momentum follows emerging crowd conviction; countertrend bets that an emotional stretch will revert. Countertrend requires stricter exits and superior risk control.
- **Market efficiency loophole**
  - When to use: deciding whether active trading can be justified.
  - How: assume prices reflect both information and crowd emotion. The active trader's opportunity is not omniscience but rational behavior when the crowd is not rational.

## Key Concepts

- **Price**: the crowd's momentary agreement about value.
- **Value**: slower-moving economic reality that can stretch far away from price.
- **Investor**: seeks large value shifts and has patience to hold.
- **Trader**: seeks shorter price swings and must define entry, target, stop, and size.
- **Gambler**: seeks action, excitement, or rescue; edge and risk control are secondary.
- **Exit-first thinking**: professionals plan how to get out before they enter.

## Mental Models

- Use the **identity test** when tempted to move a stop: if the trade can no longer be explained by the original setup, it has become a different activity.
- Think of **price vs. value** as an elastic relationship: profitable swings often arise when emotion stretches price away from reasonable value.
- Use **rational minority vs. emotional crowd** when markets accelerate: fast movement can create opportunity, but only for traders who already have rules.

## Anti-patterns

- **Changing the game mid-position**: entering as a short-term trade and converting to a long-term investment after a loss.
- **Trading for stimulation**: placing trades because the market is open, not because the setup has positive expectancy.
- **Countertrend without exit discipline**: trying to catch reversals while lacking a stop, target, or size cap.
- **Believing analysis is enough**: market opinion without psychology and money management is incomplete.

## Key Takeaways

1. Name the activity before committing capital.
2. A good trade has a reason to enter and a preplanned reason to exit.
3. The market's emotional component is the active trader's opportunity.
4. The crowd can be exploited only if your own behavior is less emotional than theirs.
5. Professionals spend more time on exits and risk than on excitement.
