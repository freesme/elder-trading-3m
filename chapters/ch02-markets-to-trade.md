# Chapter 2: 在什么样的市场中交易

## Core Idea

The market you choose must fit your capital, experience, temperament, and data access. A valid opinion is not enough; the instrument must have enough liquidity and volatility without exceeding your risk budget.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Liquidity + volatility selection filter**
  - When to use: before adding any instrument to a watchlist.
  - How: require easy entry/exit and enough price movement to cover costs, stops, and targets.
- **Instrument fit**
  - When to use: choosing among stocks, futures, options, or index products.
  - How: match complexity and leverage to experience. Beginners should prefer slower, liquid instruments where normal noise does not violate the risk plan.
- **Research depth over breadth**
  - When to use: building a watchlist.
  - How: follow a small set deeply before expanding. Familiarity with an instrument's behavior is a practical edge.

## Key Concepts

- **Liquidity**: the ability to enter and exit without excessive slippage.
- **Volatility**: the size of price movement available for profit and risk.
- **Slippage**: execution loss between intended and actual price.
- **Leverage**: magnifies both opportunity and ruin; most dangerous when the trader focuses only on upside.
- **Continuous futures contract**: a stitched data series used to analyze longer-term futures behavior.
- **Real-time data**: useful only when the trading style and skill require it; otherwise it often increases impulsiveness.

## Mental Models

- Use **instrument suitability** when tempted by a dramatic market: the best opportunity is useless if normal movement is too large for your account.
- Think of **watchlist size as cognitive leverage**: too many names dilute attention and worsen execution.
- Use **market personality** as an earned advantage: repeated observation improves the feel for normal vs. abnormal movement.

## Anti-patterns

- **Choosing by excitement**: selecting the fastest or most famous market because it looks lucrative.
- **Ignoring transaction costs**: trading instruments whose spread, commission, or slippage overwhelms the setup.
- **Premature futures or options trading**: using complex products before mastering basic risk control.
- **Real-time screen addiction**: watching ticks without a plan, which turns analysis into stimulation.

## Key Takeaways

1. Trade only instruments with adequate liquidity and volatility.
2. New traders should start with markets whose normal noise is survivable.
3. Fewer markets, studied deeply, usually beat broad unfocused scanning.
4. Futures require special awareness of contract value, delivery months, and historical ranges.
5. Real-time data is a tool for execution, not a substitute for planning.
