# Chapter 3: 第一步

## Core Idea

Trading preparation is a business setup problem: reduce avoidable costs, choose tools deliberately, and build a decision process that turns excessive information into a small set of executable choices.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **External obstacle audit**
  - When to use: before live trading or when performance disappoints.
  - How: review commissions, spreads, slippage, data quality, broker reliability, software, market hours, and account structure.
- **Analysis-to-decision funnel**
  - When to use: after feeling overwhelmed by market information.
  - How: compress news, fundamentals, charts, and indicators into a repeatable decision tree: long, short, or stand aside.
- **Fundamental + technical confirmation**
  - When to use: for longer trades where economic drivers matter.
  - How: use fundamentals to define background pressure and technicals to time entry/exit.

## Key Concepts

- **Trading costs**: commissions, slippage, fees, spreads, and opportunity cost from poor execution.
- **Information overload**: the state where analysis expands but decisions do not improve.
- **Decision procedure**: a written routine that turns analysis into a trade, no-trade, stop, and target.
- **Basic setup**: reliable hardware, software, data, broker access, and record templates.

## Mental Models

- Use **business overhead** when evaluating costs: a small recurring leak can erase the edge of many trades.
- Think of **analysis as a filter, not a museum**: the purpose is not to collect every fact, but to decide what action is justified.
- Use **probability not certainty** when planning: the market gives uncertain outcomes, so every plan needs protection.

## Anti-patterns

- **Overanalysis paralysis**: adding more indicators or reports instead of deciding what to do.
- **Broker or tool dependency**: treating outside voices or software outputs as authority instead of inputs.
- **Ignoring calendar risk**: holding positions through known announcements without reducing or defining risk.
- **Confusing preparation with prediction**: being prepared means knowing the response, not knowing the future.

## Key Takeaways

1. Control the parts of trading that are controllable: costs, tools, records, and rules.
2. The purpose of analysis is an executable plan.
3. Technical analysis is broadly portable, but it still requires judgment and risk control.
4. Fundamental analysis is most useful on longer horizons and should be timed technically.
5. Every trade should support the two goals: make money and learn.
