# Chapter 5: 技术分析

## Core Idea

Technical analysis is a compact language for reading crowd behavior through price, volume, and indicators. The goal is not to use every tool, but to combine a few compatible tools into a decision system.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Price as crowd vote**
  - When to use: interpreting bars, support, resistance, and indicators.
  - How: treat open, high, low, and close as footprints of amateurs, professionals, bulls, and bears.
- **Five-bullet toolkit**
  - When to use: building an indicator set.
  - How: combine moving averages, price channels, MACD histogram, Force Index, and one selected oscillator or Elder-ray.
- **Trend plus oscillator pairing**
  - When to use: avoiding false signals.
  - How: use trend-following tools for direction and oscillators for timing within that direction.

## Key Concepts

- **Open**: first consensus, often shaped by overnight orders and less-informed participants.
- **High**: the limit of bullish power in the chosen bar.
- **Low**: the limit of bearish power in the chosen bar.
- **Close**: the final consensus and often the most institutionally important price.
- **Trendline**: direction and emotional slope of the crowd.
- **Support/resistance**: zones where prior commitment creates expected reactions.
- **False breakout**: a move beyond a visible level that traps the crowd and often reverses.
- **Kangaroo tail**: a long protruding bar that signals rejection of an emotional extreme.
- **Price channel**: a practical way to locate overvaluation/undervaluation inside a swing.
- **MACD histogram**: a momentum and reversal tool based on the distance between MACD lines.
- **Force Index**: price change multiplied by volume, linking movement to participation.
- **Elder-ray**: bull power and bear power relative to an EMA.
- **Stochastic**: oscillator showing where the close sits within a recent range.

## Mental Models

- Use **technical indicators as instruments, not answers**: each measures a specific market feature and has failure modes.
- Think of **channels as emotional boundaries**: upper channel reflects optimism, lower channel reflects pessimism.
- Use **divergence as warning, not automatic entry**: it says power is weakening, not that risk can be ignored.

## Anti-patterns

- **Indicator hoarding**: adding tools until every desired opinion can be justified.
- **Parameter fitting to preference**: changing settings to make charts agree with the trader's wish.
- **Single-indicator trading**: treating one tool as sufficient for entry, exit, and risk.
- **Chasing extremes**: buying near oscillator danger zones or shorting deep oversold conditions without context.

## Key Takeaways

1. Price bars encode crowd behavior; indicators clarify different parts of that behavior.
2. Use a small, stable set of tools long enough to understand their strengths and failures.
3. Pair trend tools with oscillators.
4. Channels help set realistic targets and grade trade quality.
5. Divergences are among the most important reversal clues, especially across multiple indicators.
