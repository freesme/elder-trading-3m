# Chapter 7: 资金管理公式

## Core Idea

Money management is the survival layer of trading. It determines whether a positive-expectancy method can compound or whether a few large mistakes will end the trader's career.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Business risk vs. unacceptable loss**
  - When to use: defining what losses are allowed.
  - How: accept small predefined losses as operating costs; classify any breach of the plan or risk limit as a true loss.
- **2% Rule**
  - When to use: before every trade.
  - How: maximum account risk per trade = account equity x 2%; position size = risk budget / per-share or per-contract risk, rounded down.
- **6% Rule**
  - When to use: monitoring total monthly risk.
  - How: stop adding risk when current month losses plus open risk reach 6% of prior month-end equity.
- **Risk-first sizing sequence**
  - When to use: translating a setup into shares/contracts.
  - How: define entry and stop first, calculate per-unit risk, then calculate allowed quantity.
- **Size escalation by performance**
  - When to use: increasing trade size.
  - How: increase only after a profitable review period; reduce immediately after a losing period.

## Key Concepts

- **Trading capital**: capital allocated to trading, not total net worth.
- **Per-unit risk**: entry price minus stop price, adjusted for direction and costs.
- **Open risk**: risk still present in positions whose stops are not beyond breakeven.
- **Positive expectancy**: a method expected to make money over many trades.
- **Optimal f warning**: mathematically aggressive sizing can cause psychologically and financially intolerable drawdowns.

## Mental Models

- Use **oxygen gauge** when considering a large opportunity: survival matters more than the apparent treasure.
- Think of **2% as single-event protection** and **6% as sequence protection**.
- Use **risk budget, not profit desire**, as the determinant of trade size.

## Anti-patterns

- **Sizing by conviction**: taking larger positions because the trade feels certain.
- **Averaging down without a plan**: increasing exposure while the original thesis is failing.
- **Overleveraging small accounts**: using margin or futures contract size that makes normal noise fatal.
- **Ignoring compounding asymmetry**: forgetting that a large percentage loss requires a much larger percentage gain to recover.

## Key Takeaways

1. Survival comes before growth and large profits.
2. The chart gives the stop; the account risk budget gives the size.
3. Skip trades that cannot fit inside 2%.
4. Stop trading for the month when 6% risk/loss limit is reached.
5. Increase size slowly after demonstrated profitability; cut size quickly after weakness.
