# Chapter 8: 有条理的交易者

## Core Idea

Good records are the trader's management system. They expose process quality, reveal recurring mistakes, and make learning deliberate instead of accidental.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Four-record system**
  - When to use: building a professional trading workflow.
  - How: maintain a trade spreadsheet, equity curve, trade diary, and next-day action plan.
- **Trade performance scoring**
  - When to use: reviewing individual trades.
  - How: score entry, exit, and whole-trade performance relative to the day's range or channel width rather than only money gained/lost.
- **Equity curve management**
  - When to use: deciding whether to maintain, reduce, or increase size.
  - How: track account equity monthly; add capital only when the curve's moving average supports it, and reduce size when slope deteriorates.
- **Action plan note format**
  - When to use: preparing next-day orders.
  - How: write weekly picture, daily picture, and exact action: entry, stop, target, order type.

## Key Concepts

- **Trade spreadsheet**: structured record of entries, exits, costs, P/L, and scores.
- **Entry score**: quality of buy or sell relative to the bar's range.
- **Exit score**: quality of liquidation relative to the bar's range.
- **Trade score**: profit captured relative to channel width.
- **Equity curve**: account value over time, ideally smooth and rising.
- **Trade diary**: visual and written record of chart context and emotions.
- **Action plan**: written premarket order instructions.

## Mental Models

- Use **records as a mirror**: the trader who refuses records is refusing self-knowledge.
- Think of **P/L as incomplete feedback**: a bad process can win, and a good process can lose; scoring reveals skill quality.
- Use **written orders as friction**: paper between impulse and execution reduces last-second changes.

## Anti-patterns

- **Shoebox accounting**: keeping confirmations but no learning record.
- **Review only losers**: winners also teach what to repeat and where luck hid bad behavior.
- **Vague notes**: writing "buy signal" instead of describing the actual weekly/daily conditions.
- **Skipping plans when busy**: treating record-keeping as optional precisely when discipline is most needed.

## Key Takeaways

1. Maintain four records: spreadsheet, equity curve, diary, plan.
2. Score trade quality independently from dollar profit.
3. Review records regularly and after a cooling-off period.
4. The equity curve is a management signal, not just a result chart.
5. Prepare orders in writing before market pressure rises.
