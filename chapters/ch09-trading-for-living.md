# Chapter 9: 以交易为生

## Core Idea

Professional trading is a disciplined work routine, not freedom from structure. The serious trader allocates time, narrows attention, uses decision trees, and measures maturity by process fidelity.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Employee-of-the-system mindset**
  - When to use: enforcing rules after repeated discretionary mistakes.
  - How: act as if an absent boss will reward adherence to the tested plan, not emotional profit-seeking.
- **ABC scoring system**
  - When to use: managing a watchlist.
  - How: each week classify markets as A = actionable tomorrow, B = possible this week, C = not actionable. Focus work on A and B only.
- **Decision tree / trading plan**
  - When to use: turning market concepts into action.
  - How: write non-negotiable rules plus judgment areas: market selection, setup type, entry, stop, target, size, and review.
- **Development stages**
  - When to use: assessing trader maturity.
  - How: beginner seeks tips and big gains; serious amateur writes plans and risk rules; professional emphasizes capital allocation, records, and steady execution.

## Key Concepts

- **Humility**: confidence in process without certainty about any single outcome.
- **Routine**: scheduled data download, review, watchlist scoring, planning, and record updates.
- **Professional workload**: research, testing, planning, execution, and review.
- **Trading plan**: a tailored framework reflecting market, strategy, account size, and personality.
- **Time allocation**: more time on high-probability markets, less on idle ones.

## Mental Models

- Use **boss test** when tempted to improvise: would a manager pay you for following this process?
- Think of **watchlist attention as capital**: spend it where a trade can realistically occur.
- Use **professionalism as repeatability**: the mature trader can explain what they will do before the market forces emotion.

## Anti-patterns

- **Freedom fantasy**: treating trading for a living as escape from schedules and accountability.
- **Tip-driven trading**: acting on outside ideas without passing them through your system.
- **Excessive watchlist breadth**: tracking too many instruments and missing the few that matter.
- **Profit target without process target**: wanting income from trading without defining time, rules, and review.

## Key Takeaways

1. Professional trading requires scheduled work.
2. Use ABC scoring to focus attention.
3. A written trading plan separates non-negotiable rules from judgment areas.
4. Humility and discipline matter more than market bravado.
5. Progression toward professionalism shows up in records and capital management.
